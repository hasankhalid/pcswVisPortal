Financial Inclusion

Increasing access to financial services is crucial for creating economic opportunities for women. But before informed policy can be designed to include women in formal financial sector in Punjab, policy makers must have access to data to know:

Guiding questions

What proportion of women own accounts at a bank or any other formal financial institution?

What is the penetration of mobile based transaction services among women?

What proportion of women have access to credit for business or other purposes?

Globally, women are believed to be 7% points less likely than men to have a bank account

Source: Demirguc-Kunt, Asli, Leora Klapper, Dorothe Singer, and Peter Van Oudheusden. 2015. “The Global Findex Database 2014: Measuring Financial Inclusion around the World.” Policy Research Working Paper 7255, World Bank, Washington, DC

ESW Survey 2018 finds that about 9% women aged 18-64 years in Punjab are financially included as compared to 24.3% men (PCSW Male perception survey). This makes women in Punjab about 15% points less likely than men to be financially included. The wide gender gap calls for urgent interventions to improve access to financial services for women.

Note: Financial inclusion is defined as either owning an account (in bank or other institution) or using mobile based transaction services.

In this section, data visualizations present key findings on financial inclusion from the ESW survey 2018. We hope that the baseline data will provide a foundational knowledge for policy makers to design effective policies in this area.

The first visualization presents data on the following:

  • Financial Inclusion | Stacked bars to the right of the visual showing:

    • Proportion of women who own accounts (in bank or other financial institutions)
    • Proportion of women who use mobile based financial transactions (and do not have bank accounts because it is possible for women to own a bank account and also use mobile phones for transactions.)

  • Access to credit | Stacked bars to the left of the visual showing:

    • Proportion of women who applied for a loan at a formal institution (for business or other reasons). This proportion does not include loans from informal sources.

The above data are also broken down by demographic characteristics, usage of technology and disability status. The bubbles on the left of the visual indicate the distribution of population across the list of characteristics.

Access to credit and financial services for women aged 18-64

Data also suggests that there is a positive correlation between financial inclusion and women’s power to make decisions and control their own cash earnings.

Guiding questions

Are financially included women more likely to have higher decision making power?

The second visualization addresses the above question by comparing the following for financially excluded and financially included women:

  • Decision making on marriage

    • Proportion of women who chose spouse themselves
    • Proportion of women who chose jointly with their husband

  • Decision making on buying assets (women aged 18-64)

    • Proportion of women who make decision about purchase of a particular asset

  • Control over cash earnings (women aged 18-64 who work as paid employees)

    • Proportion of women who control their cash earnings themselves
    • Proportion of women who control their cash earnings jointly with husbands (limited to married women)

The visualization has two sets of circles. Orange circles represent financially excluded women whereas the purple ones represent financially included women. The circles' area denotes thier actual share in the population. The differences are shown by horizontal directed lines pointed towards the circles for financially included women.

Note: Blue lines represent positive difference between values for financially included and financially excluded. Red lines represent negative difference between values for financially included and financially excluded.
